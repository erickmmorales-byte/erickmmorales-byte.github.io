title: "On Rereading Books You Hated in School"
date: 2025-04-22
excerpt: "I hated The Great Gatsby at seventeen. The characters seemed vapid, the symbolism felt forced, and the ending made me angry in a way I couldn't articulate. I picked it up again last month and couldn't put it down."
---

The book hadn't changed, of course. But I had accumulated enough failed ambition, enough parties attended out of obligation, enough green-light-across-the-water yearning, to finally understand what Fitzgerald was pointing at. The book is about the lies we tell ourselves to keep going, and at seventeen I simply hadn't told enough of them yet.

This is, I think, one of the strongest arguments for keeping a reading list that bends back on itself. Books read too early become fixed objects in memory — snapshots of your misunderstanding. Books reread become conversations, and the conversation changes as you change. You bring different questions; the text offers different answers.

I now keep a short list of books I was assigned in school that I plan to revisit one per year. So far: *Gatsby*, *Lord of the Flies*, and *Their Eyes Were Watching God*. Each has been more interesting the second time. I suspect the same will be true of the person reading them.
